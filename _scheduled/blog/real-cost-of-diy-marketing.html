The $100/hr Mechanic Doing $20/hr Marketing: The Real Cost of DIY

15 Sep 2026 · 5 min read

If your labour rate is $100 an hour, every hour you spend fumbling through a Google Ads dashboard, tweaking Facebook targeting, or trying to write SEO content is an hour billed at effectively $20 an hour once you account for the mistakes, the missed opportunities, and the time it takes to learn a skill you'll use once a month.

  • DIY marketing isn't free, it's just paid for in your time instead of a fee.
  • Platforms change constantly, keeping up requires ongoing time, not a one-off learning curve.
  • Mistakes in ad accounts are often invisible until you've already burned budget.
  • The real question isn't "can I do this myself", it's "should my time go here instead of the bay".

The Math Most Workshop Owners Skip

Three hours a week on marketing, at a $100 labour rate, is $1,200 a month in lost billable time. Compare that to what a specialist would charge to do it properly, and the DIY route often isn't the cheaper option it looks like on paper.

It's Not Just Time, It's Missed Opportunity

Every hour spent guessing at Google Ads settings is an hour not spent on a paying job, or on a customer conversation that could have led to a referral. The opportunity cost is often bigger than the direct time cost.

The Hidden Cost of Getting It Wrong

A poorly configured ad account doesn't just underperform, it can actively waste budget on the wrong searches, the wrong locations, or the wrong audience, sometimes for months before anyone notices. That's money spent with nothing to show for it, on top of the time already lost.

What "Doing It Yourself" Actually Costs Over a Year

Add up the weekly hours, the labour rate, and the wasted ad spend from trial and error, and DIY marketing often costs more over a year than a specialist would have charged, while producing worse results.

Common Questions

Isn't DIY still worth it when I'm just starting out?

It can be, for the very basics like setting up a Google Business Profile. Paid ads and ongoing SEO are usually where the time cost outweighs the savings.

How do I know if my time would genuinely be better spent elsewhere?

Track how many hours a week you're actually spending on marketing tasks, then multiply by your labour rate. That number usually settles the question.

What if I can't afford an agency right now?

There's a middle ground, focused, limited-scope support can be far cheaper than a full retainer, while still saving you the bulk of your time.

Can I learn enough to do it properly with a course or two?

You can learn the basics, but platforms change constantly. The ongoing time cost to stay current is the part most people underestimate.
