Misfiring Leads? Check These 3 Things Before You Blame the Algorithm

8 Sep 2026 · 4 min read

It's easy to blame "the algorithm" when leads slow down. It's rarely the whole story. Before assuming Google or Meta changed something against you, there are three things worth checking first, because more often than not, that's where the real problem sits.

  • Check your budget and bid settings before assuming the algorithm has turned against you.
  • Check your landing pages for anything broken, slow, or outdated.
  • Check your response times, a lead that waits too long often books elsewhere before you even call back.
  • Algorithm shifts happen, but they're rarely the first or only explanation for a slow patch.

1. Check the Basics of the Campaign Itself

Has your daily budget quietly run out early each day? Has a bid strategy been switched without anyone noticing? Simple account-level issues cause more "lead droughts" than any algorithm update. This is the first place to look, and often the fastest fix.

2. Check What Happens After the Click

A campaign can be performing perfectly and still produce zero leads if the landing page it sends people to is broken, slow to load, or missing a clear call to action. Click through your own ads regularly, on mobile, and check what a real customer actually experiences.

3. Check How Fast You're Actually Responding

A lead form filled out at 9pm and answered the next morning has usually already booked with someone else. Response speed is one of the biggest, and most fixable, factors behind a "lead drought" that has nothing to do with the ads themselves.

When It Actually Is the Algorithm

Sometimes it genuinely is a platform-side change, seasonal competition increase, or market shift. But that should be the fourth thing you check, not the first, because the first three are usually where the real answer is hiding.

Common Questions

How often should I check these three things?

Weekly at a minimum during active campaigns, daily during your busiest periods when a missed lead costs the most.

What counts as a "fast" response time?

Within five minutes is the gold standard. Within the hour is acceptable. Anything beyond that starts costing you bookings.

Could it be a mix of all three, not just one?

Absolutely, and it usually is. These issues compound, a slow landing page and a slow follow-up together do more damage than either alone.

How do I check this without a marketing background?

Run through your own ad and booking process as if you were the customer, that alone will surface most of the obvious issues.
