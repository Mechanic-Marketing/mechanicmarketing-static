Tune-Ups vs. Overhauls: When to Pivot Your Marketing Strategy

29 Sep 2026 · 5 min read

Every mechanic knows the difference between a car that needs a tune-up and one that needs a full engine overhaul. Marketing works the same way, and most workshops reach for the overhaul, a whole new strategy, a new agency, a new platform, when what they actually needed was a tune-up.

  • A tune-up fixes specific, identifiable issues without touching what's already working.
  • An overhaul is a full strategic reset, usually only needed when the fundamentals themselves have changed.
  • Jumping straight to an overhaul often throws away campaigns and data that were still valuable.
  • Most "my marketing isn't working" problems are tune-up problems, not overhaul problems.

Signs You Need a Tune-Up

Cost per lead has crept up gradually. One or two campaigns are underperforming while others are steady. Your website converts fine but one specific service page is lagging. These are targeted issues that need targeted fixes, not a full rebuild.

Signs You Genuinely Need an Overhaul

You've expanded into new services or locations your current strategy was never built for. Your entire lead volume has collapsed across every channel at once. Your last strategy was built around a version of your business that no longer exists. These are structural problems, and they need a structural response.

Why Workshops Default to the Overhaul

A slow month feels urgent, and urgency pushes people toward dramatic action. But scrapping a whole strategy over one soft patch often means losing months of learning, data, and momentum that a targeted fix could have preserved.

How to Tell the Difference Fast

Ask whether the problem is isolated (one channel, one page, one campaign) or system-wide (everything, everywhere, all at once). Isolated problems get a tune-up. System-wide problems, especially tied to real business changes, get an overhaul.

Common Questions

How do I know which one my workshop actually needs?

Start by mapping out what's underperforming specifically. If you can name it, it's probably a tune-up. If everything feels broken at once, it's worth a bigger conversation.

Is an overhaul always more expensive than a tune-up?

Usually, yes, both in cost and in the time it takes to rebuild momentum, which is why it's worth confirming you actually need one first.

Can a tune-up turn into an overhaul partway through?

Sometimes, if the diagnosis uncovers something bigger. That's normal, the key is starting with the smaller, targeted fix first.

How often should a workshop expect to need either one?

Minor tune-ups regularly, as part of normal optimisation. A genuine overhaul is rare, usually tied to major business changes.
